# What "Single Batch" Really Means in Mezcal

You see it on the label of almost every craft spirit now: *small batch, single batch, limited release.* On most shelves it means very little. In mezcal, made the old way, it means almost everything.

## A batch is a moment, not a recipe

Industrial spirits are built to taste identical forever. A master blender's job is to erase difference — so the bottle you buy today matches the one from five years ago. That is a feat of engineering, and it is the opposite of what we do.

A true single batch of mezcal is the output of one distillation run, from one harvest of agave, roasted in one earthen pit, fermented with the wild yeast that happened to be in the air that week, and distilled in the same copper still by the same hands. Change any of those and you change the spirit. So a single batch can never be made twice. When it is gone, it is gone.

That is why, for a maestro mezcalero, "single batch" is not a boast. It is a statement of fact and a limit at the same time.

## Why it can't be repeated

Four things make every batch its own:

- **The agave.** Espadín cut from one hillside, in one season, carrying the particular sugars of that year's rain and sun.
- **The roast.** Days in a stone-lined pit over wood fire. The smoke is never identical twice.
- **The ferment.** Open-air, wild yeast. No two ferments behave the same.
- **The hands.** A maestro reads the still by smell and instinct, cutting the heads and tails by judgment, not by meter.

Multiply those variables and you get a spirit that is, quite literally, unrepeatable. The next batch will be its own thing — good, but different.

## What this means for you

It means scarcity in mezcal is not invented for the label. It is built into how the spirit is made. A single-batch release is finite from the first bottle. The good ones don't get restocked; they get remembered.

It also means the most interesting bottles are often the ones you can't simply add to a cart.
